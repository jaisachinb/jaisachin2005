Questions I keep returning to

A space where I write about things I am exploring: open questions in usable security and HCI, observations from reading, and ideas that have not yet found their way into a paper. These are working thoughts, not finished arguments.

Do security warnings actually change behavior, or do they just shift blame?
Research Question
April 2026

Most security warnings are designed by systems teams, not behavioral scientists. When a browser flags an insecure connection, or an app requests camera access, the user is nominally "informed." But informed consent in security contexts assumes a level of mental model clarity that decades of research on warning habituation suggests is simply not present.

The question I keep coming back to is this: are warnings designed to help users make better decisions, or are they designed to transfer legal and moral responsibility away from the system designer and onto a user who lacks the context to act meaningfully on the information?

The two goals produce very different designs. A warning built for genuine decision support would be contextual, timed appropriately, worded to match the user's actual mental model, and calibrated to interrupt only when the decision genuinely matters. A warning built for blame-shifting needs only to be present and technically legible, regardless of whether it changes anything.

I suspect most warnings in the wild are closer to the second than the first. If that is true, the implication is not just a design critique but a structural one: the incentives of system builders are misaligned with the informational needs of users in ways that no amount of UI polish will fix without changing who bears responsibility for security outcomes.

Can the AMC framework extend from hardware attackers to online threat actors?
Exploration
March 2026

During my work at the University of Queensland, I used the Awareness, Motivation, and Capability (AMC) framework to study attackers of patented IC mechanisms. The framework is compelling because it treats adversaries as decision-makers operating under bounded rationality, not as omniscient agents with unlimited resources. You model their threat not by worst-case capability but by what they are likely to actually know, want, and be able to do.

Since then I have been thinking about whether AMC transfers cleanly to online threat actors. Phishers, social engineers, and credential stuffers operate in very different ecosystems. Their awareness is shaped by dark web markets and open-source intelligence rather than academic literature and patent filings. Their motivation is often financial and commoditized rather than competitive or ideological. Their capability is frequently purchased or rented rather than developed.

The interesting question is whether the commoditization of attack tooling collapses the capability dimension of AMC in online contexts. If you can buy a working phishing kit for twenty dollars, capability is nearly constant across attacker populations. What differentiates successful attackers from unsuccessful ones is then primarily awareness and target selection. That shifts where defenders should focus: not on raising the capability bar (encryption, rate limiting) but on degrading attacker awareness, which is a much less studied problem.

If the capability bar is effectively zero, usable security becomes fundamentally a problem of information asymmetry rather than technical robustness.

I do not have a clean answer here yet, but I think the framing is productive and worth developing.

Privacy as a felt experience, not a policy property
Note
February 2026

Privacy research often begins with policy frameworks: what data is collected, by whom, under what legal basis, retained for how long. These are important questions. But users experience privacy not as a policy state but as a felt sense of exposure or safety.

There is a substantial gap between the technical or legal definition of a privacy violation and whether a person subjectively experiences harm. A system can be fully GDPR-compliant and still generate deep feelings of violation. A system can collect data far beyond what its privacy policy discloses and produce no felt harm at all, if users simply do not know and would not have an emotional response if they did.

This gap matters for design. If you optimize only for technical compliance, you will build systems that are legally defensible but experientially alienating. If you optimize only for felt experience, you risk ignoring real harms that users cannot perceive. The interesting design problem is in the intersection: how do you surface real privacy risks in ways that connect to users' existing felt sense of exposure without generating so much anxiety that people disengage entirely?

I think usable privacy research needs to take subjective privacy experience much more seriously as a primary variable, not a secondary one to be measured after the technically correct design has already been determined.

When does AI assistance become AI dependency in security operations?
Research Question
January 2026

Security analysts increasingly rely on AI systems for anomaly detection, threat classification, and incident triage. There is reasonable evidence that AI assistance improves detection rates in controlled settings. There is far less research on what happens to human skill and judgment over time when AI handles the routine cases reliably.

Automation bias is well-documented in aviation, medicine, and process control: operators who work alongside reliable automated systems tend to under-monitor, over-trust, and lose the practiced skill to intervene effectively when the automation fails. The failure modes are often the highest-stakes moments, which is precisely when human judgment matters most.

I suspect analogous dynamics are developing in security operations centers. If an AI system accurately flags 95% of threats, analysts will reasonably adapt their attention accordingly. The 5% the system misses will then encounter analysts whose threat-recognition muscles have atrophied through disuse.

The design question is not just "how accurate is the AI?" but "what kind of analyst does working with this AI produce over time?" These are very different evaluations, and I am not aware of longitudinal research on human capability degradation in AI-assisted security roles that approaches the rigor we have in aviation human factors.

On reading Helen Nissenbaum: contextual integrity as a design constraint
Exploration
December 2025

I have been reading Nissenbaum's work on contextual integrity and thinking about what it would mean to operationalize it as an actual design constraint rather than an analytical framework. The core claim is that privacy violations are not fundamentally about the quantity of information disclosed but about whether information flows match the norms of the context in which information was originally shared.

Medical information shared with a doctor flows appropriately to a specialist consulted for a second opinion. It does not flow appropriately to an employer, even if the employer has a legitimate interest in employee health. The violation is not disclosure per se but a mismatch between the contextual norm under which information was shared and the context into which it has moved.

This is analytically powerful, but it creates an immediate design question: how do you build systems that track contextual norms at the level of individual users across different social contexts? Norms are not stable, not universally shared, and not always explicit even to the people who hold them. A system that tries to enforce contextual integrity mechanically risks either being so permissive it fails to protect anything, or so restrictive it becomes unusable.

I think the right framing is not to automate contextual integrity but to design for contextual legibility: systems that make it easy for users to perceive when an information flow is happening and whether it matches their sense of the appropriate context. The enforcement remains with the user; the system's job is to make the relevant information available in a form that allows a meaningful judgment to be made.
